# La Divina Commedia — A Canto by Canto Commentary

A narrative and academic web-based commentary on Dante Alighieri's *Divine Comedy*, covering all three canticles: *Inferno*, *Purgatorio*, and *Paradiso*.

## About

This project presents a canto-by-canto prose summary and literary commentary on Dante's *Divina Commedia* (c. 1304–1321), the foundational work of Italian literature and one of the supreme achievements of Western poetry. Each canto receives a substantive summary that goes beyond plot synopsis to address the poem's theology, allegory, political content, literary sources, and the human drama of the individual souls Dante encounters.

The commentary is written for a general educated reader — someone who may not have read the poem in its entirety but who wants to engage seriously with its ideas, its structure, and its remarkable cast of characters. It assumes no knowledge of medieval Italian, Scholastic philosophy, or 13th-century Florentine politics, though it does not shy away from these subjects when they illuminate the text.

## The Three Canticles

### Inferno
*c. 1304–1308 · 34 Cantos*

Dante descends through the nine circles of Hell on Good Friday, 1300, guided by the Roman poet Virgil. The *Inferno* is organized around a moral taxonomy of sin derived from Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*, moving from the sins of incontinence (lust, gluttony, avarice, wrath) through violence to fraud and treachery. The commentary covers the famous encounters — Francesca, Farinata, Brunetto Latini, Ulysses, Ugolino — and examines Dante's use of *contrapasso* (the correspondence between sin and punishment), his political rage against Florence and the papacy, and the poem's sustained theological argument.

### Purgatorio
*c. 1308–1320 · 33 Cantos*

Dante ascends the mountain of Purgatory, organized around the seven capital sins. The *Purgatorio* is the most humanly warm of the three canticles: souls here are moving toward the light, not fixed in eternal darkness, and the poem's tone shifts from dread to hope. The commentary traces Dante's philosophical development — Virgil's great lecture on love as the root of all virtue and vice, the poetic manifesto of the *dolce stil novo*, the extraordinary conversations with Statius and Forese Donati — and follows Dante to his reunion with Beatrice in the Earthly Paradise.

### Paradiso
*c. 1314–1320 · 33 Cantos*

Beatrice guides Dante through the nine celestial spheres and into the Empyrean, where he has a direct, wordless vision of God. The *Paradiso* is the most theologically ambitious and, for many readers, the most demanding of the three canticles. The commentary works through Aquinas's praise of Francis, Cacciaguida's prophecy of exile, the apostolic examinations on faith, hope, and love, and the final ascent into the light that defeats all metaphor — ending on the word *stelle* (stars), as do all three canticles.

## Design

Each canticle has its own distinct visual theme reflecting its content:

- **Inferno** — Deep black backgrounds, crimson and ember tones, fire and darkness
- **Purgatorio** — Dark ink background with warm gold accents, dawn light emerging
- **Paradiso** — Cream and parchment backgrounds, radiant gold, the light of noon

The site uses accordion-style canto cards: click any canto header to expand its commentary. An "Expand All" button opens every canto at once for reading or printing.

## Viewing

Open `index.html` in any modern web browser. No build tools, server, or dependencies are required beyond an internet connection for the Google Fonts stylesheet.

## Sources and Approach

The commentary draws on the following scholarly traditions and translations:

- The Italian text of the *Petrocchi* critical edition
- Translations by Allen Mandelbaum, Robert Hollander, and Charles Singleton
- Commentary traditions of Sapegno, Singleton, and Hollander
- Theological sources including Aquinas's *Summa Theologiae* and the works Dante himself cites

The commentaries are original prose, written to convey both the narrative drama and the intellectual architecture of each canto. They aim to be accurate without being pedantic, and interpretively engaged without being reductive.
